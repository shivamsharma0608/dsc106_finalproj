BUT — Section 3 of 6

It killed the traditional big man.

Centers and power forwards once dominated through post scoring and rebounding. Today they're expected to shoot threes, pass out of pick-and-roll, and space the floor. The skillset required to play inside has been completely rewritten.

TAKEAWAY — Section 6 of 6

Did the revolution make basketball better?

The numbers changed. The game changed. The players changed. Whether the soul of basketball got better or worse — that's a question the data can illuminate but not answer for you.

1
3-point attempts tripled from the mid-90s to 2024, reshaping every position and every team's identity.
2
True Shooting % hit all-time highs in 2023 — the modern NBA is statistically the most efficient version of basketball ever played.
3
But midrange artistry nearly died. Isolation scoring, post play, and low-post defense have been largely engineered out of the game.
4
Legends look different. Era adjustment reveals both undervalued and overvalued players from the 90s — the lens you use changes everything.

So — did the NBA get better?

Prototype Writeup

Initial Prototype — Progress Report

① What have you done so far?
We have established the full six-section narrative structure of our explorable explanation, following the and-but-therefore framework. The hero section, navigation, and overall visual identity of the site are complete, giving the project a cohesive editorial look using Playfair Display typography and a navy-gold palette. Visual 1 (the animated line chart showing league averages over time) has been built by our teammate using real Basketball Reference data, with a dropdown to toggle between 3PA, pace, PPG, and midrange attempts, annotated with the Curry era inflection point. Visual 2 (the shot zone court hex map, this page's interactive section) is implemented in D3.js and driven by real columns from our team_stats_scoring_rs.csv — specifically PCT_PTS_2PT_MR for midrange frequency, PCT_FGA_3PT for 3-point frequency, and PCT_PTS_PAINT for paint frequency — with a season slider producing animated transitions. Visual 3 (the big man radar chart) has a static mockup complete. Sections 4, 5, and 6 have their narrative text and structural scaffolding in place, ready to receive D3 visualizations.
② What will be the most challenging part to design, and why?
The most challenging section to design will be Section 5 — the era translation tool — because it requires defensible statistical methodology, not just visualization. Converting 1990s raw statistics into modern equivalents demands applying pace adjustments, league eFG% baseline corrections, and positional usage shifts in a way that is academically sound enough to be credible but simple enough to explain in one sentence of tooltip text. If the methodology is wrong or oversimplified, the entire "THEREFORE" conclusion of our story collapses. A secondary challenge is Section 2's court visualization: while the slider interaction works with aggregate zone data, the visual would be significantly more accurate with shot coordinate data rather than zone-level percentages — and sourcing coordinate data at scale requires either the NBA Stats API or a proprietary dataset we currently don't have access to.
